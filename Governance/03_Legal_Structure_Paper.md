# Legal Structure Paper

**Status:** Version 0.1 Working Draft

## Purpose

To consider potential legal structures capable of supporting the From Wildfires to Regeneration Framework's long-term governance and operations.

## Scope

A discussion paper considering structural options and their alignment with the Framework's governance principles. This paper does not constitute legal advice, does not reach legal conclusions, and does not recommend a jurisdiction-specific course of action.

## 1. Introduction and Purpose

The Founding Manuscript identifies legal structures as one of the principal open questions to be addressed before the Framework can be implemented at scale, alongside Custodianship Agreements and long-term governance. This paper opens that discussion. It does not settle it: no legal structure has yet been agreed, and none should be assumed from anything written here.

## 2. Legal Structure Options

Comparable long-term stewardship and regeneration initiatives typically adopt one of a small number of structural approaches, including (without preference or recommendation):

- a foundation or charitable entity;
- a non-profit association;
- a cooperative or member-based structure;
- a hybrid public–private partnership; or
- an agreement-based model, built primarily around Custodianship Agreements between landowners, Custodians and the Framework, without necessarily requiring a single incorporated entity to hold every relationship.

**Placeholder — requires committee decision:** which, if any, of these options — or some other structure — best suits the Framework has not yet been evaluated or decided.

## 3. Considerations Across Jurisdictions

The Framework's stated ambition, set out in the Executive Board Paper, is to be practical, scalable and exportable beyond its first deployment in Salento. A legal structure adopted for that first deployment may not translate directly to other regions or countries.

**Placeholder — requires committee decision:** no jurisdiction-specific legal analysis has yet been undertaken, in Italy or elsewhere. Any such analysis should be obtained from qualified legal counsel in each relevant jurisdiction before the Framework is deployed there.

## 4. Alignment with Governance Principles

Whatever legal structure is eventually adopted should give effect to, rather than compromise, the principles set out in the Governance Framework — in particular, that custodianship precedes ownership, and that contribution by Strategic Partners and Patrons does not confer control over the Framework's regenerative purpose.

## 5. Regulatory and Compliance Considerations

**Placeholder — requires committee decision:** land use, environmental, agricultural, tax and other regulatory considerations relevant to the Framework's activities have not yet been assessed and require specialist advice.

## 6. Risks and Limitations

Operating without a settled legal structure carries risk to funding continuity, liability, and the Framework's long-term continuity, considered further in the Risk Management Framework. This paper does not resolve those risks; it exists to open the discussion the Founding Manuscript identifies as necessary.

## 7. Preliminary Observations

The choice of legal structure remains an open, unresolved decision for the Framework's governing participants. It is suggested — without concluding the matter — that the choice be informed by the principles in the Governance Framework and made with qualified legal counsel before further external partnership agreements are finalised.
