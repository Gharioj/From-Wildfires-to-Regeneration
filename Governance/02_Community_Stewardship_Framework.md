# Community Stewardship Framework

**Status:** Version 0.1 Working Draft

## Purpose

To set out how the From Wildfires to Regeneration Framework engages, respects and shares stewardship with the communities affected by wildfire and involved in long-term regeneration.

## Scope

Covers community participation, consultation and consent, the recognition of local and indigenous knowledge, and shared responsibility across regeneration activities. Does not cover the Framework's formal governance structure, which is addressed in the Governance Framework.

## 1. Introduction and Purpose

The Founding Manuscript describes the Framework's origin as "a new model of custodianship of our land, uniting landowners, Custodians and the local community in a shared path of regeneration, biodiversity, innovation and prosperity." This Community Stewardship Framework sets out that model in more detail.

This document should be read alongside the Governance Framework (Document 01), which establishes the Custodian, landowner and local community roles at a principle level. This document is the primary reference for how those roles and the wider stewardship relationship are intended to operate in practice.

## 2. Principles of Community Stewardship

- **Custodianship before ownership.** As set out in the Governance Framework, stewardship of the land is held in trust rather than owned outright by any single participant.
- **Collective capability.** The Founding Manuscript is explicit that regeneration "will not depend on a single organisation or a few people, but on the ability of the local community to work together, each according to their own capacity, skills and responsibility."
- **Multi-purpose intervention.** Consistent with the Framework's founding principle that every intervention should address more than one problem, stewardship activity is expected to serve prevention, regeneration, biodiversity and community benefit simultaneously wherever possible.
- **Continuity of knowledge.** Stewardship should actively protect the transmission of knowledge, skills and experience to future generations, rather than treating it as a by-product of other activity.
- **Voluntary, invited participation.** The Framework was presented as a working proposal, open to critical review and improvement by landowners, farmers, professionals, associations and the local community — not as a settled model imposed on them.

## 3. Roles and Responsibilities

- **Landowners** make land available to the Framework and participate in shaping how it is protected and regenerated on their property.
- **Custodians** form a distributed network of stewards present on the land. During the period of highest wildfire risk, Custodians undertake fire surveillance. For the remainder of the year, they contribute to environmental monitoring, biodiversity recording, sampling where appropriate, practical regeneration work (including new planting and restoration), documentation of the land's evolution, and engagement with the local community.
- **Local community, associations and professionals** contribute expertise and lived knowledge of the land, participate in reviewing and improving the model, and share in its outcomes.

**Placeholder — requires committee decision:** the specific terms on which landowners and Custodians participate (including any compensation, tenure or duration arrangements) are intended to be set out in a Custodianship Agreement. That agreement has not yet been finalised.

## 4. Participation, Consultation and Consent

The Founding Manuscript was presented explicitly as "a working basis," with an invitation to landowners, farmers, professionals, associations, the local community and all interested parties to analyse it critically, improve it where appropriate, and contribute to its rapid implementation. Land is intended to be included in the Framework only with the landowner's agreement, formalised through a Custodianship Agreement.

**Placeholder — requires committee decision:** a formal mechanism for ongoing community consultation and consent, beyond the initial invitation to review the founding proposal, has not yet been agreed.

## 5. Local and Indigenous Knowledge

The Founding Manuscript frames long-term regeneration as depending on knowledge that "is not lost" — describing the aim not simply as teaching technique, but as rebuilding "a living relationship with the land, made of observation, practice, responsibility and continuity." This includes existing local knowledge of terrain, olive groves, water and fire behaviour, alongside techniques the Framework introduces (such as Hugelkultur and syntropic agriculture).

**Placeholder — requires committee decision:** no formal process has yet been defined for recording, crediting or otherwise formally recognising local knowledge contributions within the Framework.

## 6. Conflict Resolution and Support

**Placeholder — requires committee decision:** the existing publication suite does not yet address how disagreements between landowners, Custodians, the local community and partners would be resolved, nor what support (training, equipment, welfare) is available to Custodians. This section requires substantive decisions not yet made and should be developed in a future version.

## 7. Review and Evolution

The Founding Manuscript invites continuous improvement directly: "If better solutions exist, let us incorporate them. If some parts can be improved, let us improve them." Consistent with the Governance Framework's accountability principles, this document should be reviewed as the Framework's measured outcomes — including the extent of the Custodian network and the participation of the local community — provide evidence of what is working.
