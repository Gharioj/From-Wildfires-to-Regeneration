**Status:** Version 0.1 Working Draft

## Purpose

To establish the principles and processes through which risks to the From Wildfires to Regeneration Framework — including environmental, operational, financial and reputational risks — are identified, assessed and managed.

## Scope

Covers risk identification, assessment, mitigation and monitoring across the Framework's activities and partnerships.

## 1. Introduction and Purpose

The Executive Board Paper opens with the challenge the Framework exists to address: wildfires increasingly threatening biodiversity, communities, livelihoods and ecosystems. This Risk Management Framework extends that same attention to risk inward, to the Framework's own governance, operations, finances and reputation, and should be read alongside the Governance Framework (Document 01), Legal Structure Paper (Document 03), and Financial Sustainability Framework (Document 05).

## 2. Risk Management Principles

- **Prevention-oriented.** Consistent with the Framework's own Prevention-first operational design, risks to the Framework itself should be identified and addressed before they escalate, rather than managed only once realised.
- **Proportionate and evidence-based.** Risk management should be proportionate to a deployment's scale and informed by the outcomes the Framework already measures.
- **Shared responsibility.** Managing risk is a shared responsibility across Custodians, Strategic Partners, Patrons and governance bodies, not the sole responsibility of any one participant.

## 3. Risk Identification and Categories

- **Environmental and operational risk.** Wildfire itself, as described in the Executive Board Paper; biodiversity loss; land degradation; and the increasing frequency and severity of climate-related events, identified in the Board Paper as a long-term structural driver of demand for the Framework.
- **Continuity risk.** The Founding Manuscript identifies land abandonment, the erosion of local knowledge and skills, and bureaucratic complexity as existing pressures on the land the Framework seeks to address.
- **Governance and legal risk.** The Framework does not yet have a settled legal structure (Document 03); operating without one carries risk to liability, continuity and the clarity of relationships between participants.
- **Financial risk.** The Framework's long-term sustainability model beyond initial Patron and Strategic Partner funding is not yet agreed (Document 05, Section 6).
- **Reputational risk.** The Legacy Recognition arrangements described in the Patron Invitation create an expectation of stewardship integrity; failure to deliver on funded commitments could affect the Framework's reputation and future patronage.

## 4. Risk Assessment and Prioritisation

**Placeholder — requires committee decision:** no formal risk register or prioritisation methodology has yet been adopted. Establishing one is recommended as part of a future version of this document.

## 5. Mitigation and Response

Mitigation is, in part, already embedded in the Framework's design: the Prevention, Early Detection and Rapid Response phases (Document 04) exist specifically to reduce wildfire risk before it escalates, and the Custodian network (Document 02) provides continuous human presence on the land. Financial risk is partly addressed through diversified funding sources (Document 05, Section 3). Governance and legal risk are addressed through the resolution of Documents 01 and 03.

## 6. Monitoring and Reporting

Risk indicators should be monitored through the same transparency and accountability mechanism set out in the Governance Framework (Document 01, Section 5) — for example, tracking area still exposed to wildfire risk alongside area already protected.

## 7. Review and Evolution

**Placeholder — requires committee decision:** this framework should be reviewed as the first deployment provides real operational evidence, but no formal review cadence has yet been agreed.
