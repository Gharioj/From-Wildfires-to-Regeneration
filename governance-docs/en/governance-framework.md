**Status:** Version 0.1 Working Draft

## Purpose

To establish the governance principles, structures and decision-making processes through which the From Wildfires to Regeneration Framework is directed and held accountable.

## Scope

Applies to the Framework's governing bodies, its decision-making processes, and its relationship to implementing partners, patrons and strategic partners. Does not cover day-to-day operational decisions, which are addressed in the Operational Governance Framework.

## 1. Introduction and Purpose

The Framework brings together landowners, Custodians, local communities, Strategic Partners and Patrons in a shared effort to move from wildfire risk toward long-term ecological and social regeneration. The Founding Manuscript identifies long-term governance as one of the principal open questions to be resolved before the Framework can be implemented at scale. This document is the first working step toward answering it.

As the Framework moves from a single founding proposal toward a model intended to be practical, scalable and exportable, its governance must hold two things together at once: a consistent methodology and set of principles that travel with the Framework wherever it is deployed, and the freedom for each deployment to be engineered for its own environmental, climatic, ecological and operational conditions. This Governance Framework sets out how that balance is directed and held accountable, both within an individual deployment and across the wider network the Framework is designed to become.

## 2. Governance Principles

- **Consistency with local adaptation.** Every deployment is engineered for the region in which it operates, but every deployment shares the same underlying principles and methodology. Local adaptation is a matter of engineering, not of governing purpose.
- **Custodianship before ownership.** Governance exists to protect the Framework's regenerative purpose and the land, communities and knowledge connected to it — not to confer control over that purpose to any single participant.
- **Proportionate governance.** Governance should be no heavier than a deployment's scale and complexity require, scaling deliberately as the Framework grows from a first regional deployment toward wider adoption.
- **Evidence-based decision-making.** Decisions are informed by the outcomes the Framework already commits to measuring — including area protected, area regenerated, and the extent and coverage of the Custodian network — rather than by assumption.
- **Continuity of knowledge.** Governance should protect the transmission of knowledge, skills and experience across generations of participants, consistent with the Framework's founding commitment to continuity.

## 3. Governance Structure and Roles

The Framework distinguishes between roles already established in its founding and strategic documents, each carrying a distinct governance relationship to the Framework:

- **Founding Global Strategic Partners** — organisations contributing a distinctive, essential capability to the Framework's Global Rapid Response model (for example, environmental technologies, early detection, aerial response, communications, construction, energy, water systems, or regenerative agriculture).
- **Regional and National Strategic Partners** — contributors of funding, technology, technical expertise, engineering, operations, research or logistics at a regional or national level.
- **Patrons** — contributors of long-term, legacy, strategic impact or transformational funding.
- **Regional Implementation Partners** — organisations responsible for delivering a deployment within a specific region.
- **Custodians** — the distributed network of stewards present on the land, responsible for surveillance during periods of highest wildfire risk and for ongoing environmental monitoring, biodiversity recording and regenerative work throughout the year.
- **Landowners and local communities** — the parties whose land, knowledge and participation the Framework exists to serve, and without whom no deployment can proceed.

Governance operates at two levels: a founding or global level responsible for the Framework's overall direction, consistency and integrity, and a regional or local level responsible for implementation suited to local conditions. The precise composition and constitution of the bodies exercising these responsibilities is not yet defined and is expected to develop through subsequent versions of this document.

## 4. Decision-Making Processes

Decisions should be made at the most local level capable of making them well, with Framework-wide questions — methodology, principles, and consistency across deployments — reserved for governance at the founding level. Operational decisions within a deployment are addressed in the Operational Governance Framework.

Major decisions affecting the Framework's direction, principles or wider adoption should be documented and capable of being traced back to the evidence and reasoning that informed them. This section is expected to be substantially developed in a future version, including how decisions are proposed, who is consulted, and how disagreement is resolved.

## 5. Accountability and Transparency

The Framework already commits to documenting and monitoring its results, including the extent of the Custodian network, fire surveillance coverage, hectares protected and regenerated, soil quality, water retention, biodiversity, and the participation of the local community. Governance accountability is built on this existing commitment: outcomes should be reported transparently to Strategic Partners, Patrons, Custodians and the local communities involved, and should inform how governance itself is reviewed and adjusted.

## 6. Relationship to Strategic Partners and Patrons

Strategic Partners and Patrons contribute capability, funding and expertise; they do not thereby acquire control over the Framework's regenerative purpose. This distinction follows directly from the founding principle that a Custodianship Agreement does more than define a relationship — it protects land, shares expertise, creates collaboration and generates mutual benefit. The same principle is intended to extend to every partnership the Framework enters into: contribution is welcomed and structurally defined, but the Framework's purpose remains held in common rather than owned by any contributor.

## 7. Review and Amendment

This Governance Framework should be reviewed periodically and revised in light of the evidence the Framework collects about its own outcomes. Amendments should preserve the founding principles set out in Section 2 while allowing governance structures and processes to adapt as the Framework moves from its first regional deployment toward wider, international adoption.
